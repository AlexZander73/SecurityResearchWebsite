Package Delivery Scams

Fake shipping alerts push people to pay tiny “redelivery fees” or reveal personal details.

  • SMS scams
  • Payments
  • Phishing

Updated 2026-03-20

What this is

Delivery scams impersonate couriers and claim a package is delayed, unpaid, or undeliverable. The message tries to move you to a fake payment or login page.

How it works

  1. A text or email mentions a package problem.
  2. A link opens a page with courier-like branding.
  3. You are asked for a small fee or address confirmation.
  4. Card data and identity details are harvested.

Why people fall for it

  • Many people are expecting packages most of the time.
  • Small fees feel believable and easy to approve.
  • The scam message often includes plausible order language.

Warning signs

  • Unknown sender IDs or random phone numbers.
  • Generic greetings instead of your name.
  • Links that do not match official courier domains.
  • Pressure to pay immediately to avoid return-to-sender.

Example scenario

You receive a text saying your package requires a $1.95 customs fee. The page looks official enough, but the domain is unrelated to the courier brand.

What to do if it happens

  1. Do not pay through message links.
  2. Check delivery status from your retailer account or courier app.
  3. If you paid, freeze the card and dispute the charge.
  4. Report the message as spam/phishing.

How to reduce risk next time

  • Track orders from trusted retailer confirmations.
  • Use virtual cards for low-trust online payments.
  • Treat tiny urgent charges as a common scam tactic.
Quick reminder: You do not need proof that something is fake before you pause. One credible red flag is enough to stop and verify.
